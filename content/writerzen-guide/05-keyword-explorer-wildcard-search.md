# 2.3 How to Use Wildcard Search in Keyword Explorer

## Overview

This lesson covers the Wildcard Search functionality in Keyword Explorer — an advanced operator that helps you discover keywords with specific intents from a very general seed keyword.

## How It Works (Mechanism)

Wildcard Search works exactly like Google's wildcard operator, which uses an asterisk (`*`) symbol. The asterisk represents a word and instructs Google to search for words that could substitute it, using proximity searches.

**Example:** Typing `how to * quora` tells Google to suggest popular words that people have typed between "how to" and "quora" in that sentence structure.

The asterisk can be placed anywhere in the search phrase.

## Creating a New Search

1. Switch to the **Wildcard tab** in the search bar before performing your search
2. Add at least one asterisk (`*`) to your search phrase
3. Choose your language (45+ available) and location
4. Click Search

**Example search:** `how to * popcorn`

Google will suggest various potential replacements for the wildcard based on real user searches — a powerful source of content ideas and customer insights.

> **Tip:** Enable Textbook Mode to see definitions when hovering over underlined text. Detailed use case explanations are also available in the Learning Hub (top right of screen).

## Keyword Data Tab

The Keyword Data table shows a complete list of keyword variations with the asterisk replaced by words or phrases to form complete search terms.

The results table has four tabs:

| Tab | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| **Total** | All variations of your search term with related metrics |
| **Exact Match** | Keyword phrases that include your keyword in the exact order written — the asterisk is replaced but the sentence structure stays intact |
| **Almost Match** | Keyword phrases that include your keyword in any order — the asterisk is replaced to fit various popular search terms, including additional words |
| **Similar Match** | Related keywords that users have also searched for alongside your keyword — useful for discovering great related terms to target |

Below the tabs, a set of filters helps you organise your results. Click any keyword to expand it and access detailed metrics.

## Benefits of Wildcard Search

- **Topic inspiration** — surfaces ideas you may not have thought of. For example, a search for `how to * popcorn` might return "how is popcorn made" with 3,600 visits per month.
- **Customer insight** — shows popular words and phrases people are actually using, keeping you up to date with new and surging search trends.
- **SEO mindset** — search is not about what people find, it's about how people think. Regularly updating your keyword lists with wildcard searches helps you find good opportunities and stay competitive.

## Use Case 1: Building a Full-Funnel Content Plan

Combine Wildcard Search with WriterZen's Golden Filter and Keyword Clustering to build a content plan that covers all search intents across the sales funnel:

| Funnel Stage | Intent Type | Content to Create |
|---|---|---|
| **TOFU** (Top of Funnel) | Informational — "how to" searches | Articles that nurture leads and build awareness |
| **MOFU** (Middle of Funnel) | Consideration — comparison and evaluation searches | Comparison articles helping users decide between options |
| **BOFU** (Bottom of Funnel) | Ready-to-buy searches | In-depth knowledge content and offers |

**Example:** A wildcard search for `how to * Toyota Camry` with the Golden Filter activated surfaces how-to keywords ideal for TOFU/BOFU content. Running the cluster function then groups these keywords automatically. A separate search for `Toyota Camry vs *` surfaces comparison keywords for MOFU content.

Creating this broad range of content and using internal linking effectively boosts topical authority for your pillar page and your site overall.

## Use Case 2: Geo-Targeting for Local Businesses

Wildcard Search is a powerful tool for local businesses looking to find and optimise a geo-targeting keyword list.

**Goal:** Find locations within your service area that you could create or optimise content for.

**Example:** A plumber in Manchester, UK runs a wildcard search like `plumber in * Manchester`. Google returns various towns within the Manchester area that fall within the service range.

From here you can:
- Create new content targeting each of these towns
- Re-optimise existing content on your site to target these locations
