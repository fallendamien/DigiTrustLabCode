# 1.2 Report Analysis: How a Topic is Demonstrated In-Depth

## Overview

This lesson takes a deep dive into the Topic Discovery results using the "nutrition plan" project from the previous lesson, covering how to work with all the data available in the interface.

## Textbook Mode and User Guide

You can switch on Textbook Mode to enable tooltips. Hovering over any underlined element displays additional context. The same information is also available in the User Guide inside the Learning Hub.

## Filter Options

The filters section allows you to refine the data to quickly choose topics that best fit your content strategy.

**Relevancy levels:**
- **Closely** — topics that are very closely related to your seed keyword
- **Medium** — a middle ground between closely and widely
- **Widely** — the broadest set of topics, stretching to those that are somewhat related

Relevancy is determined by how often topics and subtopics are searched for in relation to the main topic. Experiment with each setting to see which works best for your research goal.

**Sort order:**
- **By Search Volume** — organises topic cards from highest to lowest search volume
- **By Relevancy** — organises topic cards from most to least relevant

## Exporting Ideas and Display Modes

- **Export button** — exports topic ideas as an Excel file
- **Cards view** (default) — displays each topic as a card
- **Mind Map view** — shows a visual of how each clustered topic connects to the pillar content

## Exploring a Topic Card

Each topic card shows:
- Main topic (top left)
- Subtopics beside and below it
- Search volume
- First few results
- A link to open the full card

Subtopic cards have a **search icon** in the top right to dig deeper into that particular topic.

A **golden star** next to a topic title indicates the topic has experienced an upward search volume trend in recent months.

### Inside a Topic Card (Show Ideas)

Clicking "Show Ideas" reveals three sections:

1. **Headlines to Consider** — top-ranking results for the search query. Use these as inspiration for your own headlines. Click the icon next to any result to visit the competing article directly.

2. **Google Suggest Insights** — similar or related searches to consider as subtopics. For example, "nutrition plan with meals" is a natural subtopic when writing about nutrition plans.

3. **Related Google Searches** — searches typically found at the bottom of Google SERPs. A valuable source for discovering additional search terms to include in your campaign.

## Adding Ideas to a List

The **Add to List** feature lets you store headlines, topics, keyword lists, and content ideas in your personal database.

To save items:
1. Check the headlines or suggestions you want to save
2. Click **Add to List**
3. Create a new list or save to an existing one

Your saved lists are accessible from the **Keyword List widget** in the top right of the screen, next to the notification and workspace dropdown.

## What Topic Discovery Does That Keyword Research Cannot

Most SEO tools focus on keywords — they tell you what words to include in your article but do not help you understand your audience at a deeper level or show you the topics relevant to a search.

Topic Discovery gives you a **cluster of topics** related to your seed keyword. This is known as a **topical map**, where each topic represents a potential article on your website. This structure:
- Organises your content plan
- Signals topical expertise to Google
- Surfaces content ideas you may not have thought of on your own

## Use Case 1: Low Authority Site Targeting Competitive Keywords

**Situation:** You have a group of short-tail, high-volume keywords but your website has low authority and cannot yet compete for them.

**Approach:**
1. Use Topic Discovery to find related or more specific subtopics you can realistically rank for
2. Take those subtopics into Keyword Explorer to review their metrics
3. Build keyword lists and cluster them
4. Publish subtopic articles and use internal linking to build authority toward your pillar page

**Example:** The search term "nutrition plan" is highly competitive. Using Topic Discovery with medium relevancy returns 42 topic ideas. Subtopics like "inflammation diet plan," "diabetic meal plan," "workout meal plan," and "deficit diet plan" are strong candidates to write about first — provided the keyword metrics are favourable.

## Use Case 2: Finding a Different Angle on an Existing Keyword Cluster

**Situation:** You already have keyword clusters to work with but your competitors are ranking for the same keywords. You need a less competitive angle.

**Approach:**
1. Take the seed keyword from each cluster and run a Topic Discovery search
2. Browse the topic cards for ideas that represent a different angle or a more specific focus
3. Look for cards with a golden star — these are seeing a recent surge in searches
4. Save promising topics and headings to a list for later

**Example:** For the keyword cluster "tall low light indoor plants," a Topic Discovery search reveals angles like:
- Best indoor plants for low light areas
- Best indoor plants for bathrooms, attics, or cellars
- Favourite or trending low light indoor plants

These narrower angles are likely less competitive than broad terms like "best indoor plants for the home," while still targeting the same underlying keywords.

## Key Takeaway

Relying on keyword research alone leaves you with overwhelming lists of keywords and no real plan. Topic Discovery organises all related data into a well-structured, easy-to-use format — combining the strengths of both keyword and topic research in the content creation process.

Run a few searches in Topic Discovery and see how quickly you can build a topical map for your next content strategy.
